## Persona 1: The Straight-Line Emigrant

**Role**: Person leaving the US for Ireland, with no firm onward plans
**Added**: 2026-03-08
**Last updated**: 2026-03-08

### Background

An American citizen or long-term US resident who has decided to emigrate to Ireland. They may be Irish-born returning home, a dual citizen activating their Irish/EU ties, or someone moving for work, a partner, or a lifestyle change. This is their first major international move. They have a household's worth of belongings accumulated over years in the US and are overwhelmed by the sheer volume of decisions — what to bring, what to sell, what's worth shipping versus replacing.

They are typically 28-55, own or rent a home with a full set of furniture and appliances, and have 2-8 months before their move date. They may have a partner and children, which multiplies the belongings and the emotional difficulty of letting things go.

### Goals

- Get clear, decisive, item-by-item guidance — not general advice articles, not Reddit threads full of contradictions
- Understand which US appliances and electronics will work in Ireland (voltage, frequency, plug type) and which are genuinely not worth the trouble
- Know what Irish customs will and won't allow, especially for items with sentimental value
- Keep total moving costs manageable — they are already spending heavily on flights, deposits, and setup costs
- Feel confident that they won't regret their decisions three months after landing

### Pain points

- Drowning in conflicting advice from expat forums. One person says bring the KitchenAid, another says don't bother, nobody explains why
- Does not understand voltage and frequency differences well enough to make safe decisions about electronics. Terrified of plugging something in and causing a fire
- The emotional weight of sorting through a life's worth of belongings while also managing visa paperwork, flights, and housing
- No clear sense of what things cost in Ireland versus the US, so they cannot judge whether shipping is worth the expense
- Worried about customs delays or fines for items they didn't realise were restricted

### Behaviours

- Works through decisions item by item or category by category, often while physically standing in the room with the item
- Frequently uses their phone during sorting sessions — the app must be excellent on mobile
- Often returns to a previous decision: "Actually, what about the stand mixer attachments too?"
- May paste a list of items compiled in a notes app and want batch answers
- Searches for specific items by name and expects Aisling to know the product (e.g. "Vitamix A3500" not just "blender")

### Needs

- **Must-haves**: Fast, decisive answers. Clear SELL / SHIP / CARRY / DONATE / DISCARD verdicts. Voltage and customs facts presented plainly. Works well on mobile.
- **Nice-to-haves**: Batch processing of item lists. Tips on where to sell specific items in the US. Rough price comparisons (US vs Ireland). A summary or checklist they can export.

### Quotes

> "I just want someone to tell me what to do with my stuff."
> "I spent three hours on Reddit and I'm more confused than when I started."

## Persona 2: The Two-Leg Planner

**Role**: Person moving US to Ireland with a firm or likely plan to continue onward to Australia
**Added**: 2026-03-08
**Last updated**: 2026-03-08

### Background

Similar demographics to the Straight-Line Emigrant, but with a critical difference: Ireland is a waypoint, not the final destination. This person is moving to Ireland first (often for work, a partner's visa situation, or as a deliberate stepping stone) and plans to relocate to Australia within 1-5 years.

This makes every decision more complicated. They are not just asking "will this work in Ireland?" — they are asking "will this work in Ireland AND then in Australia, or will I just have to deal with it again in two years?" They carry the mental load of two sets of customs rules, two voltage standards (Ireland 230V/50Hz, Australia 230V/50Hz — same voltage but different plug types and drastically different biosecurity rules), and two shipping cost calculations.

The Australia leg is often the source of the most anxiety. Australia's biosecurity regulations are notoriously strict — wooden items, food products, outdoor equipment, and anything that has touched soil can be seized or require expensive treatment. This persona has usually read enough horror stories about Australian customs to be genuinely nervous.

### Goals

- Get advice that accounts for BOTH legs of the move, not just the first one
- Understand the difference between Irish customs rules and Australian biosecurity rules — and know which items will be a problem at each border
- Avoid shipping something to Ireland only to discover it cannot enter Australia later
- Minimise the total number of times they buy-sell-ship the same category of item
- Make smart "rent vs own" and "temporary vs permanent" decisions for the Ireland period, knowing it's not forever

### Pain points

- Cannot find advice that addresses a two-leg move. Everything online is written for single-destination moves
- Australian biosecurity anxiety is real and specific: "Can I bring my hiking boots?" "What about the wooden cutting board my dad made?" "Will they x-ray my books?"
- The timeline uncertainty makes planning hard — they might be in Ireland for 18 months or 5 years, and the advice changes depending on which
- Feels like they are optimising for two contradictory sets of rules simultaneously
- Storage costs in Ireland eat into their budget if they keep things "for Australia" but don't use them daily

### Behaviours

- Asks about items with explicit two-leg framing: "Should I ship this to Ireland if I'm going to Australia in two years?"
- Needs Aisling to proactively flag when an item is fine for Ireland but problematic for Australia (or vice versa)
- May ask Aisling to give separate verdicts for each leg
- More likely to think in timelines: "If I'm only in Ireland for two years, is it worth shipping a couch?"
- Often asks about the Australian biosecurity rules in hypothetical terms because the move is not imminent

### Needs

- **Must-haves**: Two-leg advice on every item. Clear flags for Australian biosecurity risks. Timeline-sensitive recommendations (e.g. "ship if you're staying in Ireland 3+ years, don't bother if it's under 2").
- **Nice-to-haves**: A comparison view showing Ireland-leg advice vs Australia-leg advice. Storage cost guidance for Dublin/Cork. An "Australia watch list" — items to re-evaluate when the second move gets closer.

### Quotes

> "I'm terrified of Australian customs. I've read they'll fine you thousands for a dirty shoe."
> "I feel like I'm playing three-dimensional chess with my belongings."
> "Nobody can tell me whether to ship stuff to Ireland when I know I'm leaving again."

## Persona 3: The Second-Leg Mover

**Role**: Person already living in Ireland (having moved from the US), now preparing for the move to Australia
**Added**: 2026-03-08
**Last updated**: 2026-03-08

### Background

This person completed the first leg — they are already in Ireland. They may have been there for anywhere from 6 months to several years. They have a mix of belongings: things they shipped from the US, things they bought in Ireland, and possibly things in storage. Now the Australia move is becoming real, and they need to go through the sorting process again, but with a different set of rules.

Their situation is distinct from the other personas in several ways. They are no longer thinking about US-to-Ireland logistics at all. Their departure country is now Ireland, not the US. Their primary anxiety is Australian biosecurity, customs duties, and whether items they already shipped once are worth shipping again. They may also have accumulated Ireland-specific items (230V appliances, furniture bought locally) that are a better fit for Australia's electrical system than their old US gear was.

They have likely used — or wish they had used — a tool like Moving Fairy for their first move. They come to the product with more experience, more specific questions, and less patience for generic advice.

### Goals

- Get decisive advice specifically for the Ireland-to-Australia leg
- Understand Australian biosecurity rules in practical detail — not government-website legalese, but "will they actually stop me for this?"
- Determine which US-origin items they shipped to Ireland are now worth shipping again to Australia versus selling in Ireland and replacing
- Navigate the emotional fatigue of sorting through belongings for the second time in a few years
- Avoid the mistakes they made on the first leg (over-shipping, under-selling, not understanding voltage)

### Pain points

- Sorting fatigue: "I cannot believe I'm doing this again"
- Unclear on whether items they already paid to ship once are now "sunk cost" or still worth moving — they conflate the original shipping cost with current value
- Australian biosecurity rules feel opaque and punitive. They have heard stories but do not know the specifics
- Some of their belongings are a mix of US and Irish origin, and they are not sure how to classify them for customs
- Their transformer (if they have one) works in Ireland — but will it work in Australia? (Answer: yes, same voltage, but different plug — they need an adapter, not a transformer. This is a common point of confusion.)

### Behaviours

- Asks highly specific, experienced questions: "I shipped my Le Creuset from the US. Is it worth shipping to Australia or should I sell it in Dublin and buy new there?"
- May want to revisit advice from their first move, or express regret about earlier decisions — Aisling should acknowledge this gracefully, not dwell on it
- More likely to ask about the logistics of selling in Ireland (where to sell, what things are worth on the Irish second-hand market)
- Engages with the app in shorter, more focused sessions — they know the drill, they just need the Australia-specific layer
- May not need the full onboarding — they already know their route. But the form is still valuable for capturing their current equipment and timeline

### Needs

- **Must-haves**: Australia-focused biosecurity and customs advice. Ability to set Ireland as departure country (when supported). Clear guidance on "ship again vs sell and replace" economics. Honest advice on what Australian customs will actually enforce vs what is technically on the books.
- **Nice-to-haves**: Irish second-hand market tips (Adverts.ie, DoneDeal, Facebook Marketplace Ireland). A "what I shipped last time" list they can re-evaluate. Encouragement — this persona is tired and needs Aisling to be especially warm.

### Quotes

> "I shipped a KitchenAid to Ireland and now I'm wondering if I ship it again or just sell the thing."
> "The Australian government website makes it sound like they'll confiscate my shoes. Is that actually true?"
> "I just need the Australia layer. I've already done the hard part once."
